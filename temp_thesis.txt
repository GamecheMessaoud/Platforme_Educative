Abstract
Programming education has become essential in today’s digital world, but Algeria
faces major challenges: language barriers, prohibitive training costs, the absence of
locally adapted platforms, and the lack of personalised support. This thesis presents
Sadeem, a bilingual gamified educational platform specifically designed for Algerian
children aged 8 to 16.
Our main contribution lies in the design and implementation of a RetrievalAugmented Generation (RAG) system that powers the intelligent pedagogical
assistant Cody, capable of providing personalised assistance in Arabic and English.
The platform offers a progressive learning path from visual programming (Blockly) to
textual code (Python, C++, Arduino), with a complete gamification system including
experience points, badges, levels and leaderboards.
The platform delivers an innovative, accessible solution (1,500 DA/month), adapted
to the Algerian context and filling the gaps of existing international platforms.
Keywords: Digital education, Programming for children, RAG, Artificial Intelligence, Gamification, Bilingual platform, STEM, Algerian context.

L’éducation à la programmation est devenue essentielle dans le monde numérique actuel,
mais l’Algérie fait face à des défis majeurs : barrière linguistique, coût prohibitif des

la programmation visuelle (Blockly) au code textuel (Python, C++, Arduino), avec

classements.
La plateforme constitue une solution innovante, accessible (1,500 DA/mois), adaptée au contexte algérien et comblant les lacunes des plateformes internationales existantes.

General Introduction
Context
Programming education has become a fundamental pillar in the training of future
generations in an increasingly digital world. It plays an essential role in developing
21st-century skills, including critical thinking, problem-solving, creativity and collaboration [1]. In the past, learning programming was reserved for university students and
IT professionals. Today, it is recognised as a fundamental skill that should be taught
from an early age, on a par with reading, writing and mathematics [2].
Algeria has a significant young population, with approximately 30% of its inhabitants under fifteen years old. This demographic reality represents both a challenge
and an opportunity for technological education. However, despite the Algerian government’s efforts to promote digital education through several initiatives, the country
still faces persistent obstacles that limit young learners’ access to quality educational
resources in programming.

Problem Statement
Several major obstacles hinder the development of programming education in Algeria:
• Language barrier. The majority of programming learning resources are available
only in English or French, creating a real barrier for young Arabic-speaking learners.
The rare Arabic resources often lack pedagogical quality and adequate technical
support.
• Absence of local platforms. No educational platform is designed specifically for
the Algerian context, with local cultural examples and references. International
platforms use culturally distant examples that do not resonate with Algerian children.
• Prohibitive cost. Private programming courses cost between 5,000 and 15,000
DA per month, making them inaccessible to many Algerian families. International
platforms require payments in foreign currencies, further complicating access.
• Geographic disparities. Training is primarily available in large cities such as
Algiers, Constantine and Oran, excluding children from rural areas and small towns.
• Lack of personalised support. Children learning alone via online resources lack
assistance when they encounter difficulties. Qualified programming teachers are
scarce and the student-to-teacher ratio remains very high.

• Lack of pedagogical continuity. Existing learning paths are fragmented, with
no clear progression from visual programming to textual code, nor integration with
electronics and robotics.
Given these challenges, the central research question of our work is: How can we
design and develop an educational platform that is accessible, culturally adapted, and
technologically innovative in order to democratise programming education for Algerian
children?

Objectives
To answer this question, our project Sadeem1 aims to develop an innovative educational platform meeting six complementary objectives:
1. Linguistic accessibility: offer a bilingual interface (Arabic/English) with native
RTL support, allowing Arabic-speaking children to learn in their mother tongue
while developing English skills.
2. Engagement through gamification: provide gamified learning inspired by successful platforms like Duolingo, integrating experience points (XP), badges, levels,
leaderboards and streaks to maintain long-term motivation [3].
3. Intelligent assistance: integrate an AI-based pedagogical assistant (Cody) using a Retrieval-Augmented Generation system [4] to provide personalised support,
available continuously, capable of answering in Arabic and English.
4. Progressive learning path: provide a structured path going from visual programming (Blockly/Scratch) to textual code (Python, C++), up to embedded electronics
(Arduino).
5. Financial accessibility: offer an economic model adapted to the Algerian context
with an affordable monthly price (1,500 DA), integration of local payment methods
(CCP, Chargily), and a STEM store for electronic kits.
6. Pedagogical interactivity: offer interactive programming laboratories, simulators
(Wokwi for Arduino), adaptive quizzes, and community spaces for project sharing.
Sadeem is the Arabic word for “nebula”: a cloud of stars in which new stars are born. We
chose this name as a metaphor for our platform, where young learners — the future stars of Algerian
technology — begin to form.

Methodology
Our development approach follows an iterative, user-centred methodology structured
in four phases:
1. Analysis: study of the Algerian educational context, comparative analysis of existing platforms, and definition of functional and technical specifications.
2. Design: definition of the layered technical architecture, data model, bilingual RTL
interface, and RAG pipeline design.
3. Development: implementation of the frontend, backend services, relational database,
and the RAG system with ChromaDB and NVIDIA NIM models.
4. Testing and validation: unit and integration tests, RAGAS framework validation
of the RAG pipeline, performance and load tests, and UX evaluation with a pilot
group of Algerian children.

Thesis Structure
Chapter 1: State of the Art and Analysis lays the theoretical and analytical
foundations. It presents the pedagogical context (e-learning evolution, computational
thinking, STEM, gamification) and the technological foundations (NLP, LLMs, the
RAG paradigm). It conducts a detailed comparative study of major platforms and
synthesises the positioning of Sadeem.
Chapter 2: Solution Design describes our main contribution: the global four-layer
architecture of Sadeem and the detailed architecture of the Cody AI module. Each
phase of the RAG pipeline is explained with its role, method, model, and parameters.
Chapter 3: Implementation, Testing and Evaluation presents the concrete realisation: development environment, implementation of the five main modules, containerised deployment, four-level testing strategy with RAGAS evaluation, and experimental
results with a pilot of Algerian children.
A General Conclusion synthesises the work accomplished, answers the research
question, discusses limitations, and proposes future perspectives.

Introduction
The teaching of programming, electronics and computational skills to children has
become a strategic stake of contemporary education. This first chapter lays the theoretical and analytical foundations of our work. We begin by exploring the pedagogical
context: the evolution of e-learning toward adaptive learning systems, computational
thinking, STEM education, and the contribution of gamification to children’s motivation. We then present the technological foundations: NLP with its child-specific
challenges, Large Language Models, and the RAG paradigm. We conclude with a critical review of related works and a comparative study that highlights the gaps relative
to the Algerian context.

1.1

Pedagogical and Educational Context

1.1.1

From Passive E-learning to Adaptive Learning

The first generation of e-learning relied on linear delivery of pre-recorded content
(videos, PDFs, static quizzes) identical for all learners. Although this approach democratised access to knowledge, it suffered from a major weakness: the absence of personalisation, leading to high abandonment rates and limited knowledge retention [5].
To overcome these limits, the field progressively evolved toward adaptive learning:
a pedagogical approach in which content, difficulty, pace and feedback dynamically
adjust to each learner’s profile. This evolution is materialised in Intelligent Tutoring
Systems (ITS), which simulate the behaviour of a human tutor [6]. An ITS typically
articulates four modules: the domain model, the learner model, the pedagogical model
and the user interface.
For children, personalisation is even more crucial. A child encountering repeated
failure without contextual help quickly loses motivation. Conversely, an over-easy
exercise produces boredom. The pedagogical challenge is to maintain the learner in
their Zone of Proximal Development [7]: the space where the task is neither too easy
nor too difficult with appropriate guidance.

Computational Thinking

Computational Thinking, formalised by Jeannette Wing in 2006 [1], is a set of
problem-solving skills presented as a fundamental competency for everyone. It rests
on four complementary pillars: decomposition, pattern recognition, abstraction,
and algorithms. This approach finds its natural anchoring in STEM education [2],
an interdisciplinary paradigm based on real-world applications.

1.1.3

Gamification in Learning

Gamification is defined as the application of game elements and mechanics in nongame contexts to increase engagement and motivation [3]. In education, it has proven
particularly effective for maintaining children’s interest [8]. Several theoretical frameworks support this effectiveness: Self-Determination Theory [9] (Deci & Ryan, 1985),
which emphasises the three intrinsic needs of autonomy, competence and relatedness, and Flow Theory [10] (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990), which describes the state of
optimal engagement when challenge matches the user’s skill level.
The main gamification mechanics deployed in modern educational platforms are
illustrated in Figure 1.1: experience points (XP) quantify progress and create a sense
of accomplishment; levels provide short-term goals; badges act as visual prestige markers; leaderboards stimulate healthy competition; and streaks reward consistent daily
practice.
Empirical studies converge on showing positive impact: learner engagement can
increase by up to 48%, course completion rates improve by approximately 36%, and
knowledge retention increases by around 25% in gamified environments [8]. Beyond raw
motivation, gamification fosters perseverance: by transforming failure into a learning
opportunity, it normalises error — a fundamental posture in learning to program.

Technological Foundations

1.2.1

Natural Language Processing

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the subfield of AI dedicated to the automatic understanding, generation and manipulation of human language. It has gone
through three major paradigms: rule-based (1950s–1980s), statistical (1990s–2010s),
and neural (since the 2010s), based on the Transformer architecture [11].
NLP plays a central role in building a pedagogical assistant for children. Two
interaction modalities are particularly relevant:

Text-based interaction. When a child types a question, the system must handle
informal and imperfect input: simplified syntax, frequent spelling errors (especially at
ages 8–12), mixed Arabic/English code-switching, and vague intentions. A robust NLP
system must perform contextual understanding [12] across multiple turns, bridging
lexical ambiguity, dialectal variation, and incomplete phrasing.
Voice-based interaction. Voice input is particularly important for the youngest
learners (ages 8–11) who type slowly. It also lowers the barrier for Arabic-speaking
children who have not mastered the Arabic keyboard. Algerian children naturally mix
Arabic and English in the same utterance, which generalist models such as Whisperlarge-v3 [13] partially handle but that specialised models such as Arabic-WhisperCodeSwitching [14] address more robustly.
Designing responses tailored for children. Research in child-directed language [15]
shows that effective communication with young learners requires simplified sentence
structure, concrete analogies grounded in the child’s daily experience, a single concept
per turn, and an explicit engagement question to keep the dialogue active. These
principles directly shape the four-block answer format imposed on Cody’s generation
prompt, described in Chapter 2.

Understanding context written by children. Children often omit the subject,

Despite their power, LLMs present critical limitations for educational use:
• Hallucinations: generation of plausible but factually false information, particularly harmful when the user is a child unable to detect the error [24].
• Curriculum ignorance: a generic LLM has no knowledge of the platform’s specific

studied.
• Safety risks: possibility of prompt-injection (jailbreaking) leading to content inappropriate for a young audience.
• Knowledge cutoff: the model only knows information from its training data, with
no automatic update.
These limitations directly motivate the RAG architectural choice described in the
following subsection.

1.2.3

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), introduced by Lewis et al. in 2020 [4],
addresses the limitations of LLMs by augmenting generation with external, controlled
knowledge stored in an indexed corpus. Figure 1.3 illustrates the paradigm: the language model’s parametric knowledge (encoded in its weights during training) is combined with a non-parametric external knowledge base (the platform’s lesson corpus),
producing grounded, up-to-date answers.
The RAG process operates in two phases:
1. Retrieval: semantic search across a curated knowledge base extracts the passages
most relevant to the user’s question.
2. Augmented generation: a prompt combining the question and retrieved passages
is sent to the LLM, which is instructed to answer strictly based on the provided
context and to acknowledge when the answer is absent.

Solution Design
Introduction
After establishing in the previous chapter the theoretical foundations and the limitations of existing platforms, this chapter is devoted to the design of the solution we
propose: Sadeem, an educational platform dedicated to the teaching of programming
and electronics to Algerian children, equipped with an intelligent bilingual pedagogical
assistant named Cody.
The chapter is organised in three parts. The first part presents the motivations
that justify the project. The second part presents the global architecture of the platform, structured in four cooperating layers. The third part is devoted to our main
contribution: the detailed architecture of the AI pedagogical module, the description
of its inputs, the explanation of each phase of its processing, and the justification of the
methodological and model choices that we made. It should be noted that this chapter
focuses on the description, the roles, the methods and the models retained, without entering into low-level technical details. The concrete implementation, the development
environment and the experimental results will be presented in the next chapter.

2.1

Motivation

2.1.1

Importance of Online Education

Online education has become a major lever for democratising access to knowledge, particularly in countries where qualified human resources are unevenly distributed across
the territory. For programming, which is a discipline learned by progressive practice,
the online format offers three decisive advantages: continuous availability, the possibility of progressing at one’s own pace, and the sharing of pedagogical resources at
minimal cost. In the Algerian context, where the geographical distribution of competent computer science teachers is unequal between large cities and inland regions, an
online platform is a natural way to bridge this gap and to offer every child, whatever
their wilaya, the same quality of educational content.

Limits of Existing Platforms for Algerian Children

The comparative study carried out in Chapter 1 revealed that existing platforms, however successful internationally, do not meet the specific needs of Algerian children. None
offers a truly native Arabic interface with right-to-left reading. None integrates payment methods accessible in Algerian dinars. The pedagogical content is built around
foreign cultural references that reduce engagement. The learning paths are most often fragmented between visual programming, textual coding and electronics. Finally,
none offers a conversational pedagogical assistant capable of helping the child in their
language, at their level, and on the precise content of the lesson in progress. These
weaknesses, taken together, leave a clear empty space that our project aims to fill.

2.1.3

Contribution of Artificial Intelligence to Education

The recent progress of artificial intelligence, in particular generative language models,
profoundly changes the possibilities of online education. Where the previous generation of platforms could only offer pre-recorded content identical for everyone, AI now
makes it possible to build personalised companions, capable of reformulating an explanation, of proposing an adapted example, of detecting an error in reasoning, and of
dialoguing patiently with the learner. For a child alone in front of their screen in the
evening, this transforms the experience: instead of a static course consumed passively,
learning becomes an interactive dialogue. This is exactly the role that we entrust to
our pedagogical assistant Cody.

2.1.4

Project Objective

Faced with these realities, we propose Sadeem, an educational platform designed for
Algerian children aged 8 to 16, combining a bilingual interface (Arabic with native
RTL support and English), a progressive learning path from visual block programming
to textual coding to embedded electronics, a complete gamification system, and an
intelligent pedagogical assistant Cody based on Retrieval-Augmented Generation.

The four layers communicate vertically: a request coming from a child in Layer 1

2.2.2

Description of the Roles of Each Layer

Layer 1 — Users Interface
The first layer corresponds to the human actors: Students (aged 8 to 16, the central audience), Teachers (responsible for the pedagogical content), Administrators
(overall functioning of the platform), and Parents (read-only access to their child’s
progress). The role of this layer is not only to display interfaces, but to define the
authorisation contract on which the entire rest of the architecture relies: every request
that enters the upper layers is attached to a user and a role, and is filtered accordingly.
Layer 2 — Application and Pedagogical Services
The second layer is the operational heart of the platform. It hosts the user interfaces (lesson pages, visual block editor, textual code editor, electronics simulator,
dashboards, gamification displays) and the services that make these interfaces alive:
pedagogical content delivery, interactive learning environments (Blockly editor, code
editor, Arduino simulator), progression and gamification (XP, levels, badges, streaks,
leaderboards), access and security management, and economic services (subscriptions,
CCP/Chargily payments, STEM store). This layer is also the one that calls the AI
pedagogical module of Layer 3 whenever the child asks a question to Cody.
Layer 3 — AI Pedagogical Module (Cody)
The third layer is our main contribution. It contains the conversational pedagogical
assistant Cody, capable of answering the questions of the child in Arabic or English,
by relying on the actual content of the lessons. Its detailed architecture is presented
in Section ??. Its role is to transform a free-text question into a useful, accurate and
pedagogical answer, while keeping several guarantees: grounded in the official course
content, adapted to the level of the child, expressed in their language, and — when the
context is too weak — preferring an honest refusal rather than an invented answer.
Layer 4 — Data and Infrastructure
The fourth layer is the foundation that supports the three upper layers: storage of
user data, lessons and transactions; storage of the pedagogical knowledge base used

Voice Pipeline

The voice pipeline converts a browser audio recording into a verified text question.
The important design decision is that speech recognition does not automatically
trigger the RAG answer. The child first sees the transcript, edits it if necessary,
and only then sends it to Cody. This avoids answering a wrong question when the
ASR model mishears a programming term.

2.4.1

Input Capture and Audio Normalisation

In the browser, the child presses and holds the microphone button. The page records
a short audio clip and sends it to POST /transcribe. The server enforces a maximum
duration using STT_MAX_AUDIO_SECONDS to keep the interaction responsive and prevent
very large uploads.
The raw browser format is converted into a standard audio representation:
• Format conversion: ffmpeg is preferred; pydub is available as a fallback.
• Target format: 16 kHz mono WAV, suitable for Whisper models.
• Loudness normalisation: pyloudnorm applies EBU R128 normalisation toward
TARGET_LUFS=-23.0, improving consistency between quiet and loud recordings.
• Silence trimming: silero-vad removes non-speech segments when ENABLE_VAD=true.

2.4.2

Language Identification and Routing

After preprocessing, Cody estimates the language profile of the utterance. The implementation uses speechbrain with a VoxLingua107 language identification model
when ENABLE_LID=true. In addition, the lingua-language-detector package is used
to detect French influence inside transcribed text, which is useful for Algerian codeswitching.
The routing objective is simple: use the model that is most likely to produce a
correct transcript for the child’s speech.
• English or general multilingual speech: routed to openai/whisper-large-v3.

• Arabic or Arabic-English code-switching: can be routed to MohamedRashad/Arabic-Whisper
• Evaluation mode: /transcribe/sync?mode=ab can run both STT models to
compare outputs.

• Levenshtein distance through rapidfuzz;

2.6

RAG Pipeline

The RAG pipeline is the mechanism that makes Cody a grounded tutor instead of a
generic chatbot. It has two sides: an indexing pipeline that prepares the lesson corpus,
and a query pipeline that retrieves evidence and generates the answer.

2.6.1

Indexing Pipeline

The lesson corpus is stored under data/documents/. It contains Scratch, Python,
and Arduino materials in PDF, DOCX, TXT, and HTML formats. The loader in
app/services/document_loader.py recursively scans the directory and processes every supported file.
Step 1: Document loading: PDF files are first processed with PyPDFLoader. If
extraction fails or returns no chunks, PyMuPDF is tried. If the file appears
scanned or image-only, the OCR fallback uses pdf2image and pytesseract.
DOCX files use Docx2txtLoader, HTML files use BSHTMLLoader, and TXT
files use TextLoader.
Step 2: Chunking: documents are split with LangChain’s RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter.
The active configuration is CHUNK_SIZE=800 and CHUNK_OVERLAP=150. Smaller
chunks improve precision; overlap preserves context between adjacent chunks.

Step 3: Dense embeddings: each chunk is embedded with nvidia/llama-nemotron-embed-1b-v2
configured as a 1024-dimensional NVIDIA NIM embedding model.

Step 4: Vector persistence: dense vectors are stored in ChromaDB under CHROMA_PERSIST_DIR=./
Step 5: Sparse index: the same chunks are stored in a BM25 index using rank-bm25.
This index is persisted as bm25_docs.pkl, allowing Cody to match exact
terms such as variable, loop, pinMode, or Arabic curriculum vocabulary.

2.7

Pedagogical Prompting and Answer Format

Cody’s prompt is designed for children, not adults. It asks the model to:
• start with a clear and simple explanation;
• use the same language as the child, Arabic or English;
• keep programming identifiers and code keywords in English;
• provide a short code example only when useful;
• end with a small activity the learner can try;
• cite retrieved chunks using references such as [1] and [2];

• refuse when the retrieved context does not contain the answer.
The current LLM is configured through NVIDIA NIM. In the project configuration, the active model is nvidia/nemotron-3-nano-omni-30b-a3b-reasoning, with
temperature=0.10, top_p=0.70, and max_tokens=400. Earlier experiments and documentation may mention Gemma; the final thesis should use the model name that is

2.8

Session Memory

Session memory is implemented but disabled by default through ENABLE_CONVERSATION_MEMORY=fals
When enabled, it remains RAM-only and has three functions:
1. Sliding window: keeps the last MEMORY_WINDOW_SIZE=4 dialogue turns.
2. Query reformulation: rewrites short follow-ups into standalone questions before
retrieval.
3. Child profile: extracts lightweight facts such as name, age, preferred language,
current topic, level, and concepts the learner struggles with.
For privacy, full conversation content is not saved to disk. The optional log stores
only truncated snippets and latency metadata for debugging and evaluation.

2.9

Evaluation

The project includes evaluation scripts for both RAG and speech recognition. The
RAG evaluation harness calls the running API over HTTP, which means the measured
system is the same pipeline used by real users. The main RAGAS evaluation uses
openai/gpt-oss-120b through Groq as the judge LLM, with nvidia/llama-nemotron-embed-1b-v2

• Faithfulness: whether the answer is supported by the retrieved chunks.
• Answer relevancy: whether the response addresses the question.
• Context precision: whether retrieved chunks are relevant.
• Context recall: whether the retrieved chunks cover the information needed for the

The AI pedagogical module combines speech recognition, bilingual language handling,

3.3.1

Orchestration and Serving

FastAPI is a high-performance Python web framework [48] used
to expose the Cody microservice on port 8000, with native async
support essential for token-by-token streaming.
LangChain (LCEL) is the leading framework for composing LLM
applications [49]. Its expression language lets us declare the RAG
pipeline as a chain of operators (retriever | reranker | prompt | LLM |
parser) with built-in streaming and tracing.
NVIDIA NIM (NVIDIA Inference Microservices) [50] provides
optimised cloud inference endpoints. Sadeem uses three: Gemma3n for generation, NeMo Embedder for vectorisation, and NeMo
Reranker for cross-encoder reranking.

3.3.2

Text Generation and Retrieval

Gemma-3n-e4b-it is Google DeepMind’s instruction-tuned
model [22] used for query reformulation, subject detection, and final
answer generation. Chosen for its strong Arabic/English bilingual
performance and affordable inference cost.
NeMo Embedder (llama-nemotron-embed-1b-v2 ) [51] converts
text passages into 1024-dimensional vectors. Used both at ingestion time and at query time; chosen for its multilingual strength on
Arabic.

Chosen for its on-disk persistence and native LangChain integration.
rank-bm25 (BM25) is a Python implementation [54] of the classical BM25 ranking function [26]. It runs in parallel with dense retrieval to catch exact keyword matches, and its results are merged
via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF).

visualisation. A specialised model is used because generalist LLMs
often produce Mermaid code that fails to render.

This work has presented the design and realisation of Sadeem, a bilingual, gamified,

In Chapter 1, we laid the theoretical foundations of the project: the evolution

In Chapter 2, we presented the design of the platform: a global four-layer architecture (Users, Application and pedagogical services, AI pedagogical module, Data
and infrastructure) and a detailed RAG pipeline for the AI module, with explicit description of every phase — document ingestion, query reformulation, subject detection,
hybrid retrieval with RRF [27], cross-encoder reranking [34], MMR diversification [35],
confidence gating, grounded generation, diagram synthesis, and session memory. Each
technological choice (Gemma-3n on NVIDIA NIM, NeMo embedder and reranker, ChromaDB, BM25, Whisper variants, Mermaid-Llama) was justified by its alignment with
our context: bilingualism, cost control, local deployment, and child safety.
In Chapter 3, we documented the implementation: a TypeScript React / Express

Perspectives
Several directions of improvement and future work emerge naturally from the limitations identified in Chapter 3:
• Expansion of the knowledge base: ingest advanced topics (object-oriented design, real-time embedded programming, robotics) and add lessons from other subjects (mathematics, physics) to broaden the platform beyond programming.
• Native mobile application: develop a Progressive Web App in the short term
and a native React Native (or Flutter) application in the medium term, given that
many Algerian children access the internet primarily through mobile devices.
• Voice-first interface: build on the existing speech-to-text foundation to offer a
voice-first mode for the youngest learners, with text-to-speech feedback from Cody
— particularly useful for children not yet fluent readers.

• Fine-tuning of an Arabic embedding model: despite the strong baseline performance of the NeMo embedder, fine-tuning on Algerian children’s questions could
further improve retrieval quality in dialect-influenced phrasings.
• Tightening of the confidence gate: systematic threshold tuning, supported by
an enlarged RAGAS test set, to reduce false-LOW refusals without re-introducing
hallucinations.
• Adaptive difficulty engine: extend the [CHILD PROFILE] block towards a true

on the child’s mastery trajectory — the long-standing goal of Intelligent Tutoring

• Teacher analytics dashboard: richer reports for teachers and parents, with classlevel overviews, early-warning of disengagement, and progress visualisations.
• Open ecosystem: progressively open the platform to independent content creators
(verified teachers, educational associations) with editorial moderation, so that the

Project Nature

Sadeem is an online bilingual (Arabic/English) and gamified educational platform
designed for Algerian children aged 8 to 16. It offers a progressive learning path –
from visual block-based programming (PictoBlox / Scratch) to textual coding (Python)
and embedded electronics (Arduino) – supported by Cody, an intelligent pedagogical
assistant powered by a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system.
Cody answers each child’s questions in their own language, grounded in official
course content, using analogies contextualised to the Algerian environment, without
transmitting children’s data to third-party providers.
The platform includes:
• A full gamification engine (XP, levels, badges, streaks, leaderboards).
• Interactive programming labs with the Wokwi Arduino simulator.
• A moderated community space.
• A STEM shop selling electronic kits payable in DZD via CCP and Chargily.
Sadeem aims to bridge the Algerian digital education gap through three objectives:
1. Bridge the digital divide – democratised access, native RTL Arabic interface,
Algerian cultural references and examples.
2. Make learning fun – gamification to sustain children’s motivation over time.
3. Provide AI companionship – Cody offers personalised 24/7 tutoring without
accessing unsafe third-party services.

Conclusion

Sadeem presents a robust business model targeting a fast-growing market. The four
pillars of its viability:
• Real and urgent problem: no platform adapted to the Algerian context (Arabic
RTL interface, local culture, DZD pricing, minors’ data compliance with Law n°1807).
• Unique value proposition: the combination of Arabic RTL interface + Cody
RAG + Arduino + DZD pricing cannot be found in any direct or indirect competitor.
• Sound financial model: recurring revenue, break-even reachable at ≈340 subscribers, fixed AI inference cost (structural advantage at scale).
• Regional expansion potential: consolidate in Algeria (Years1–2), then Maghreb
(Tunisia, Morocco), then wider Arabic-speaking Middle East (Years3–5+).
The Algeria Digital 2030 strategy and the Ministry of Knowledge Economy offer a
high-multiplier B2G opportunity: a single institutional contract could bring Sadeem
hundreds of thousands of users.
